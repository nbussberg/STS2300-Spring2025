We will use the following R packages for this activity.

library(ggplot2)
library(Lock5Data)

For this activity, well use a dataset called RestaurantTips from the Lock5Data R package. The data was collected by the owner of a bistro in New York and contains 157 bills at the restaurant over a two week period. Lets explore the relationship between the size of the bill, called Bill, and the size of the tip, called Tip. Both variables are measured in dollars.

Im going to read the data into R and call it tips to shorten the name a bit.

tips <- RestaurantTips
  1. Suppose we want to create a scatterplot comparing the size of the bill and the tip size. Which variable should be the explanatory variable and which should be the response variable? Why?

Answer: We will use the size of the bill (explanatory variable) to help us predict/explain the tip size (response variable). People use the bill size (which comes first) to determine their tip (which comes second).


  1. Make a scatterplot comparing the bill size and the tip size. (Bonus: Consider adding a theme and making the labels nicer with labs())
ggplot(tips, aes(x = Bill, y = Tip)) +
  geom_point() +
  theme_bw() +
  scale_x_continuous(labels = scales::label_dollar()) +
  scale_y_continuous(labels = scales::label_dollar()) +
  labs(title = "Comparing Bill Size and Tip Amount at a NY Bistro",
       x = "Bill amount",
       y = "Tip size",
       caption = "Data from Lock5Data R package")


  1. What do you notice about the relationship between the two variables?

Answer: Tip size tends to go up as the bill goes up. However, there are a few values further away from most of the others (usually above them). The relationship follows a straight line pattern pretty well.


  1. Lets use R to calculate a simple linear regression line for this data. Write the formula for the line after you run your R code. Ive used R Markdowns math mode to show you how you can write the general formula. You will just need to replace \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) with the numbers from your equation. You could also replace y and x with the variable names if youd like.
tips.lm <- lm(Tip ~ Bill, data = tips)
tips.lm

  1. Would it be a good idea to use this data to predict the tip size for a $200 bill at this restaurant? Why or why not?

Answer: Not necessarily. The biggest value in our data set was a bill of $70.51. Its possible that tipping habits differ when the bill becomes that large.

  1. You can add a simple linear regression line to your scatterplot by adding geom_smooth(method = "lm", se = FALSE) as another layer to your ggplot object. Try this out below. Can you find the observation whose residual we just calculated on the graph? (Note: You will need to respecify your x and y aesthetics in geom_smooth() or move them to the ggplot() function instead.)
ggplot(tips, aes(x = Bill, y = Tip)) +
  geom_point() +
  geom_smooth(method = "lm", se = FALSE) +
  theme_bw() +
  scale_x_continuous(labels = scales::label_dollar()) +
  scale_y_continuous(labels = scales::label_dollar()) +
  labs(title = "Comparing Bill Size and Tip Amount at a NY Bistro",
       x = "Bill amount",
       y = "Tip size",
       caption = "Data from Lock5Data R package")
## `geom_smooth()` using formula = 'y ~ x'


  1. Interpret the estimated y-intercept in context of this problem. Is this an extrapolation or an interpolation?

Interpretation of y-intercept: The predicted tip is -$0.29 for a bill of $0.00 at this bistro.

Extrapolation or interpolation?: This is an extrapolation (we cant have a bill size of $0), and the result is clearly impossible.


  1. Interpret the estimated slope in context of this problem.

Interpretation of slope: The predicted average tip goes up by $0.18 for every additional dollar spent on the bill (at this bistro in New York).


  1. Does your estimated slope make sense based on what you know about tipping?

Answer: Yes! Standard tipping practices are generally to tip 15-20%, and this is in line with that.
